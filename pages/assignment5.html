"Introducing the iPhone 11"

iPhones Sell Ideologies

Persuasion is an art that nearly everyone practices. The core cause of all rhetoric and argument is to persuade an opposing party. This cannot be done without appealing or acknowledging the commonplaces and ideologies that those of the opposing party may hold.

While many people are opinionated and are not afraid to voice their ideas and beliefs for all to hear and see, such methods typically end in conflict with others, resonating amongst those who hold the same ideologies but widening the void with those who do not. This sort of argument is most commonly seen in today’s social and political struggles, perhaps the first type of debate thought of when mentioning the term 'argument'.

However, there is another form of persuasion which is more relevant to today’s world than politics and social movements: advertisements. Selling products is by far the most common practice of persuasion, one could not walk down a single street without viewing an advertisement of some sort arguing for its product or service. In fact, advertisements have become so commonplace that people subconsciously adopt the arguments they present.

Impact of Advertisements

For instance, if an ad on TV claims that Mucinex is the superior brand of cough syrup due to whatever comical or statistical reasoning it gives, most may pay no attention to it. That is, until they are sick and decide to purchase some over-the-counter relief, which is when they see the Mucinex bottle and recognize it as at least “good”. Of course, they may not actually buy it, but they end up comparing their chosen product with Mucinex to see which is better. Subconsciously, even if Mucinex failed in selling its product, it succeeded in setting its product as the standard for the viewers’ purchase. Their commonplace on cough syrup changed to “Mucinex is a good standard for comparison”.

Similarly, companies like Apple have been practicing persuasion for over a decade, and unlike the theoretical case with Mucinex, Apple has been incredibly successful. By now, it seems clear that the creators of products and its advertisements have impacted society’s ideologies and commonplaces in some way, subconsciously or not. It is this art that Apple has perfected repeatedly with each of their iPhones, masterfully demonstrated in the 'Introducing iPhone 11’ ad, which utilizes a structure that connects with the audience, demonstrates a feature set which blows them away, and ultimately reflects upon why Apple is such a successful brand today.

Targeted Ad Structure

The iPhone 11 ad structure manages to connect with the audience and present exactly what this new phone could do in their daily lives. People carry their phones with them everywhere they go; almost no exception, it is a lifeline that connects people to society. If people’s phones were to fail on them in the middle of the day, it would seriously hamper their productivity and social networking, leaving them ignorant to all that is happening around them (in a global sense).

Apple’s iPhone 11 ad acknowledges this ideology behind the lifeline that is the smartphone and presents a scenario of a day in which the iPhone is always ready to support the user, or as the ad puts it, “Ready for action”. The phone is shown to wake the user1, fall off car seats and tables without a scratch, jostle in a purse, become the victim of spills, and still end the day with battery to spare. Such reliability is a crave amongst smartphone users, a fulfillment of the ideology for a strong connection to today’s global society that some mock older generations of phones for failing to realize2 (mostly due to their poor battery life or lack of resistance).

Thus, Apple assures its audience that the product it is selling is one that holds the same ideology for day-long reliability and that those who buy its phone will fulfill it.

Targeted Features and Presentation

Along with structuring its ad strategically, Apple made sure the new features of its phone would tie in with the everyday lives of those watching it, introducing new commonplaces for what a phone should have to be useful to those who buy it. These new features, including dual cameras and ultrawide photos, “slofies”, faster Face ID, the “fastest chip in a smartphone”, “night mode”, as well as many others are blended into people’s days in integral ways throughout the ad.

As mentioned before, Apple reinforces the ideology for a reliable lifeline by presenting the superior resistance and battery life of the iPhone. What Apple also does is create completely new commonplaces and connect them to this ideology of the ultimate lifeline, all through the presentation of the phone’s new features.

In the same way it praised its telephoto lens in the iPhone 7 as a tool of master photo creation, Apple hails the ultrawide lens in the iPhone 11, depicting it as an element every phone should have due to its usefulness in impromptu scenarios, like taking photos of a child in a grocery store. The ad also presents the idea of “slofies”, or slow-motion selfie videos, in the same way, coining the term specifically to relate to the selfie-obsessed generation known for their colloquial style.

A faster FaceID allows people to access their phones in inconvenient positions, the use of the fastest processor (the A13 Bionic) results in undisturbed gaming, and “night mode” enables users to capture moments in dark environments, all reinforcing the ideology of reliability in a phone and adding respective features as commonplaces or standards that every phone should have.

Selling Ideologies and Commonplaces

From one look at the advertisement, it’s no wonder Apple has record sales on every iPhone release and maintains such a loyal consumer base despite the growing competition from Android phones. Apple is not a company that sells products, it is a company that masters the idea of selling commonplaces and ideologies.

From the start, the first iPhone revolutionized the way people view a phone, with the later introduction of the App Store changing the way people use one (Pierce and Goode). Many products try to accomplish what Apple has done, but the loyalty of their consumer base who more than ten years ago bought into the iPhone’s revolutionary ideology is stronger than any other.

Whenever a new iPhone is released, whether it really is revolutionary or just evolutionary, Apple ensures two things: the consumer’s ideology of the perfect phone (or lifeline) will be met, and the features of the new phone will introduce uses of a smartphone that sets the standard for all others. This is the case of Mucinex but raised to a much greater degree.

Author and organizational consultant Simon Sinek best described it with his theory of the Golden Circle: he asserted that successful companies focus on why they exist and why others should care about them, before launching into how they build their product and what they sell (Sinek). Apple sells its beliefs, its ideologies and commonplaces, before its products. It is this alluring factor that not just subconsciously, but notably alters the ideologies of society: for consumers, competitors, and everyone in between.

The iPhone 11 ad is only one of countless ventures towards this result that Apple has made throughout its existence.

Conclusion

Ultimately, whether people really own their ideologies or simply copy ones that companies instill through their products and advertisements is difficult to say. iPhones are only the beginning, almost every product influences people, from technology to medicine to even chairs.

Yes, the evolution of the office chairs people work on every day have influenced society’s ideology of them, all a result from the ways in which designers chose to create and sell them.

Whether those who are passionate about their beliefs are willing to accept this truth is meaningless in the eyes of those who profit from the aftermath, the companies like Apple who manipulate the commonplaces of the world to fill their coffers. No matter what opinion either side of a political or social movement may have, it isn’t much of a stretch to say that both sides purchase and carry a reliable lifeline to society known as the iPhone.
